A Remembrance

What Makes
NYOS, NYOS.

A small collection of the traditions, rituals, and quiet magic that drew our families here in the first place — and the heart we hope to carry forward.

Compiled from a message by Elizabeth Zimmermann

A note to fellow parents

Our family chose NYOS because we love this school deeply — and like so many of you, we care about protecting what makes it special. Over the last few years, things have shifted. Some of those shifts are easy to feel but hard to name.

Rather than speculate, a few of us started asking questions and looking back at the rituals and traditions that made NYOS feel like NYOS. What follows isn't a wish to return to the past. It's an invitation to remember — and to imagine how we carry these things forward, reinvented for where we are now.

Because NYOS belongs to all of us.

Five Things We Remember

Small rituals, big roots.

01

The Lottery Feeling

When getting in felt like winning something rare.

Waitlists used to stretch for years. Families would cry — actually cry — when their child's name was finally called. There was a sense that you had been let into something, and that something was worth protecting.

Today, even some elementary grades aren't full. For a school that once felt like a lottery ticket, that's a meaningful shift worth paying attention to.

Culture & Identity
02

The PALS Program

A bus, a driver, and the bridge between big kids and little ones.

Back in the Kramer days, Miss Constance would drive the bus between the high school on Lamar and the elementary on Kramer, bringing the older students to spend time with the younger ones. It wasn't just transportation — it was mentorship. It was the older kids becoming someone for the younger kids to look up to.

"We were hopeful that when we merged onto one campus, PALS would be even easier to embrace. We'd love to see it return."
Mentorship One Campus, One Family
03

The Silly Goat Parade

Coach Bliss, his goats, and shirts we made just for the occasion.

Every year at the end of school, Coach Bliss would bring his goats to campus. The kids made matching shirts. They paraded behind the goats around the entire campus, laughing the whole way. It was completely silly and completely magical — the kind of thing kids talked about all summer.

Mr. Alec, an elementary teacher, has goats too. Maybe he's the next Goat Parade leader. The tradition doesn't have to look exactly like it did. It just has to keep its heart.

Joy & Whimsy
04

Seniors vs. Teachers Kickball

A May tradition. A small dare. A sendoff.

Every May, in the closing weeks of the school year, the seniors squared off against the teachers in a kickball tournament. Could they finally beat the people who had taught them? Sometimes yes, sometimes no — but always, it was a sendoff that felt like ours.

Senior Tradition
05

The Kramer Garden

Beautiful, lush, and tended by every elementary student.

The garden at Kramer was a small green miracle — a place every elementary kid had a hand in. It taught patience, responsibility, and the deep pride of watching something you cared for actually grow. It was also one of the most visible symbols of what a community classroom could be.

Hands-On Learning Community
Looking Forward

This isn't about going backward.
It's about not losing the heart of why we came.

Share your memories. Share your ideas. The traditions don't need to look exactly the way they used to — they just need to be cared for, brought forward, and reinvented for the school we are today.
